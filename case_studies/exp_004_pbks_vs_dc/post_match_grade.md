# Post-Match Grade: Punjab Kings vs Delhi Capitals
## 2026-05-11, HPCA Stadium, Dharamsala

**Result:** Delhi Capitals won by 3 wickets (with 6 balls remaining)
**Memo directional view:** PBKS slightly favored. Season quality gap provides direction; PBKS's 3-match losing streak and 6-8 Dharamsala record significantly moderate the edge; the market at 60.5% likely overprices PBKS.
**Band:** 49-61% PBKS / 39-51% DC — Result **at the boundary of the band** (DC won; the lower bound was 49% PBKS / 51% DC, so the outcome is barely captured. A comfortable 3-wicket win with an over to spare suggests the true probability was at or below the band's lower bound.)

## Reasoning Grade: B+

### What the memo got right

- **The contrarian pushback against the market was the memo's single most valuable contribution, and it was vindicated.** The market had PBKS at 60.5%. The memo's midpoint was ~55%, and the Skeptic's base-rate midpoint was 54-55% — both substantially below the market. DC won. The 5.5pp gap between the memo's center and the market was in the correct direction: the market overpriced PBKS. The memo explicitly stated "the market at 60.5% appears to overanchor on the full season and underweight the recent deterioration." This was the right read. The memo arrived at this by applying the Skeptic's strongest challenge: PBKS's last-8 record of 4-4 (50%) was more informative than the full-season 6-3.

- **PBKS's 3-match losing streak was correctly identified as the strongest signal against the market price.** The memo stated: "PBKS's 60% includes a 3-match losing streak as their most recent form... The market at 60.5% appears to overanchor on the full season." PBKS lost again, extending to a 4-match losing streak. The last-8 window of 4-4 (50%) predicted PBKS's trajectory far better than the season-long 6-3 (60%). The Skeptic's anchoring check showed the losing streak warranting a -5 to -8pp discount from the log5-implied 63%, and this was the correct magnitude.

- **PBKS's 6-8 Dharamsala record was correctly used to negate home advantage — Reflection Log Entry 1 applied directly.** The memo set home advantage at 0pp, citing PBKS's losing record at their own Dharamsala ground. This was validated: PBKS lost, extending to 6-9 at Dharamsala. The memo explicitly credited Entry 1: "When venue-specific results contradict structural priors, discount the prior by at least 50%. Here the contradiction is stark." This is the second consecutive match (after Exp 3's Chinnaswamy/Raipur distinction) where the venue-specific record was more predictive than generic home advantage.

- **The dead-rubber analysis was appropriately calibrated and the Skeptic's "liberation" hypothesis was validated.** The memo capped the dead-rubber effect at 1-2pp and noted DC's superior away record (3-2, 60%) as a counterweight to the "DC won't try" narrative. The Skeptic argued "nothing to lose teams in IPL have historically produced surprises because pressure is entirely on the opponent." DC played with visible intensity: Axar's 56(30) was a captain-leading-from-front performance, Miller scored 51(28), and the debutant Madhav Tiwari delivered 2/40 + 18*. DC made 5 changes (exactly the rotation Axar hinted at) and the fresh faces performed. The memo's explicit instruction — "DO NOT treat DC's 'season is over' quotes as 'DC will not compete'" — was correct.

- **DC's XI uncertainty was correctly identified as the single biggest unknown.** The memo stated: "DC's actual XI composition and effort level is the single biggest unknown." DC made 5 changes from their last XI (Nissanka, Rana, Rizvi, Nigam out; Porel, Parakh, Miller, Tiwari, Nabi in). This was the most radical rotation of any team in the experiment series. The memo correctly anticipated this as a plausible scenario and framed it as the "What Would Change This View" trigger: "If DC's confirmed XI includes 3+ changes from the last match." The identification of the key uncertainty was precise.

- **Venue data contamination was correctly handled for the third consecutive experiment.** The stats_snapshot was keyed to Mullanpur (0 matches), and the memo correctly excluded it, relying exclusively on manually sourced Dharamsala data. This is now a well-established strength of the pipeline.

- **Pace conditions symmetry (Entry 6) was correctly applied.** The memo stated: "Conditions that aid one team's seamers equally aid the other's." Starc took 2/57 for DC; Arshdeep took 2/21 for PBKS. Both pace attacks contributed. The net effect was indeed near-neutral, as the memo predicted.

### What the memo got wrong

- **The directional call was wrong: PBKS "slightly favored" but DC won by a comfortable margin.** DC chased 211 with 6 balls to spare and only 7 wickets down. While the memo's conviction was "Low" and the midpoint was close to a coin flip at ~55%, the directional framing was incorrect. The memo would have been more accurate if it had treated the match as a genuine coin flip (50-50) rather than maintaining a slight PBKS lean.

- **The "What Would Change This View" scenario for DC rotation was too binary.** The memo stated: "If DC's confirmed XI includes 3+ changes, this would signal genuine rotation over competition, pushing the band upward by 2-3pp toward PBKS 54-64%." DC made 5 changes — triggering this scenario — but the band should have moved DOWN, not up. The memo assumed rotation = weaker XI, but DC's rotation was BOTH experimental AND competitive. The new players (Miller 51, Tiwari 2/40 + 18*, Nabi's finishing six) performed better than the players they replaced. The framework failed because it assumed a monotonic relationship between rotation and weakness. Per Entry 3, the replacement XI should have been assessed for quality, not just for the fact of being replacements. Miller is a proven international finisher; Tiwari turned out to be a genuine all-round talent. The "rotation = downgrade" assumption needs to be replaced with "assess the replacement XI's ceiling as well as its floor."

- **The KL Rahul matchup dominance was correctly caveated but still overweighted.** The memo allocated -2 to -3pp for Rahul's matchup advantage vs PBKS bowlers (SR 217+ vs Arshdeep, 0 dismissals in 23 balls). Rahul scored 9(8), caught by Jansen off Arshdeep — exactly the "one dismissal in the next 10 balls" scenario the Skeptic warned about. The memo noted the small-sample caveat but still applied the adjustment. When samples are this small (23 balls, 13 balls), the adjustment should be capped at 1pp rather than 2-3pp — the variance swamps the signal.

- **PBKS's own personnel changes were under-analyzed.** The memo modeled DC's rotation extensively but did not anticipate PBKS dropping Lockie Ferguson for Ben Dwarshuis (IPL debut). None of the predicted PBKS XIs included Dwarshuis. PBKS's bowling, nominally a strength (4 quality seamers), was weakened by this change. Yash Thakur, brought as impact sub, went for approximately 55 runs from 4 overs (economy ~13.75). The bowling change shifted PBKS's death-overs quality materially downward. Entry 3 (model the replacement XI) was applied to DC but not reciprocally to PBKS — a systematic blind spot.

- **The scoring environment was under-estimated.** The memo noted conflicting first-innings averages (160 all-time vs 209 since 2023) and correctly discounted the 209 figure as "tiny sample, distorted." But the actual first innings was 210/5, closer to the "since 2023" figure. The second innings chase of 216/7 produced the highest successful chase in IPL history at Dharamsala. The altitude effect (thinner air, ball travels further) combined with a flat pitch created a high-scoring environment that the all-time average of ~160 significantly underestimated. The more recent trend was more informative, and dismissing it may have distorted the pitch model.

### What was missed

- **DC's toss decision to field first was validated, and the memo underweighted its informational content.** The memo treated DC's field-first choice as a mild PBKS tailwind (+1 to +2pp), reasoning that bat-first teams win 64% at Dharamsala. But DC captains AT THE GROUND had information about dew and conditions. DC chased 211 successfully with an over to spare — the dew factor materialized as the memo acknowledged was possible. The memo's framework correctly identified "captains at the ground have information advantages over historical aggregates" (via the Skeptic) but then still applied a +1 to +2pp PBKS toss adjustment. When a captain's informed choice contradicts a weak historical trend (n=14, wide CI), the informational value of the choice should dominate the historical aggregate.

- **David Miller's inclusion was flagged by one source (InsideSport) but discounted.** The Source Quality Clerk rated "David Miller in DC XI" as "single source, no corroboration" and recommended discounting. Miller scored 51(28) — one of the two match-winning innings. This is a genuine dilemma: the source WAS speculative and single-sourced, so discounting was defensible. But it reveals a limitation of the pipeline: when speculative predictions turn out to be correct, the very mechanism that protects against noise also filters out signal. No clear rule change follows — the discount was procedurally correct — but it highlights that speculative XI predictions sometimes contain real information from insiders.

- **The compound effect of PBKS's 4-match losing streak on team morale and fielding quality was under-modeled.** Hopes himself flagged catching/fielding as a concern after the third loss. The memo noted this but did not translate it into an adjustment. PBKS's fielding quality under pressure — a team that "let themselves down with catching" and is now facing must-win pressure while sliding — is a different risk than static season quality would suggest. The memo treated "must-win motivation" as a +1pp PBKS boost, but the evidence (Hopes' own concern, 3 straight losses) pointed in the opposite direction: pressure was degrading PBKS's performance, not enhancing it.

### Skeptic Assessment

The Skeptic's work was the strongest component of the entire evidence chain for the second consecutive experiment — and in this match, the Skeptic was more accurate than the memo.

**Strengths:** (1) The base-rate midpoint of 54-55% was substantially more accurate than the market's 60.5%. The Skeptic correctly identified PBKS as overpriced. (2) The "PBKS 3-match losing streak / last 8 is 4-4" challenge was the single most important analytical contribution — it correctly identified that PBKS's true quality was closer to 50% than 60%. (3) The dead-rubber challenge was validated by DC's performance. (4) The "nothing to lose teams produce surprises because pressure is entirely on the opponent" observation was prescient — DC played with freedom while PBKS crumbled under the weight of a must-win. (5) The pace symmetry application prevented an analytically unjustified PBKS bowling bonus. (6) All nine DO/DO NOT instructions for the Synthesizer were correct.

**Weakness:** The Skeptic correctly identified the contrarian case ("PBKS may be overpriced at 60.5%") but then hedged with "counter-contrarian: deep liquidity ($101K) means efficiency." The memo followed this hedge, settling on a ~55% midpoint as a compromise between the Skeptic's ~54-55% and the market's 60.5%. A more assertive Skeptic would have pushed the midpoint to 52-53% and potentially flipped the directional call. When the Skeptic's own analysis produces a 3-9pp gap between the base-rate estimate and the market, and the analysis is internally consistent, the Skeptic should not retreat to "but the market is deep" — deep liquidity is necessary but not sufficient for efficiency, especially when the market may be anchoring on stale full-season records.

### Decisive Factor

The match turned on two axes. First, PBKS's inability to capitalize on 210/5 — Starc's consecutive-ball dismissals of Stoinis (1) and Shashank (0) in the penultimate over prevented what could have been 220-225. Second, and decisively, the Axar-Miller partnership of 64 off 34 balls rescued DC from 74/4 — a position where the match appeared lost. Madhav Tiwari's all-round debut (2/40 + 18*) and Ashutosh Sharma's 24(10) as impact sub closed out the chase. The memo's core analytical insight — that PBKS were overpriced and DC had underappreciated upside — was correct, but the specific mechanism (captain + experienced finisher performing under zero pressure) was not anticipated in its scenario modeling.

## Lessons for Reflection Log

- **When a team captain publicly concedes the season and hints at lineup rotation, model the replacement XI for BOTH ceiling and floor — not just as a unilateral downgrade.** DC made 5 changes and won. The memo assumed "3+ changes = rotation over competition = PBKS band moves up 2-3pp." But DC's replacements included David Miller (proven international finisher) and Madhav Tiwari (who delivered 2/40 + 18*). The "rotation = weakness" assumption is a systematic bias. Dead-rubber rotations can produce BOTH a weaker XI (less cohesion, untested players) AND a stronger one (fresh legs, no pressure, players auditioning for retention). The scenario modeling must assess both tails.

- **When the Skeptic's base-rate analysis diverges from the market by 3+pp and the memo's own analysis supports the Skeptic's direction, do not split the difference with the market — lean toward the base-rate estimate.** The market had PBKS at 60.5%. The Skeptic's midpoint was 54-55%. The memo compromised at ~55%. DC won. Deep liquidity is a weak argument for market efficiency when the gap is explained by specific, identifiable anchoring biases (full-season record vs recent form, generic home advantage vs venue-specific record). "The market is deep" should not override internally consistent analytical findings.
