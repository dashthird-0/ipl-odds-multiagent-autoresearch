# Post-Match Grade: Royal Challengers Bengaluru vs Kolkata Knight Riders
## 2026-05-13, Shaheed Veer Narayan Singh International Stadium, Raipur

**Result:** Royal Challengers Bengaluru won by 6 wickets (RCB 194/4 in 19.1 overs chasing KKR 192/4 in 20 overs). Virat Kohli 105*(60), Angkrish Raghuvanshi 71(46), Rinku Singh 49*(25). Kartik Tyagi 3/32, Bhuvneshwar Kumar 1/34. Rain delayed start by 75 minutes; no overs lost.
**Memo directional view:** RCB modestly favored. Market overpriced RCB by ~3.5pp. Midpoint ~54% RCB vs market's 57.5% RCB.
**Band:** RCB 46-62% (midpoint ~54%) — Result **within band.** The memo's conditional scenario ("If RCB win toss and bowl first: band shifts to RCB 56-62%") activated exactly. With Varun Chakravarthy ruled out (+1-2pp RCB) and Finn Allen starting (-1-2pp RCB), the post-realization conditional estimate is approximately RCB 57-62% (midpoint ~59%) — above the market's 57.5%. The memo's unconditional 54% midpoint was appropriately below the market because it probability-weighted all toss outcomes; its conditional framework produced the more accurate estimate for the realized scenario.

The memo discarded all Chinnaswamy data and rebuilt the analysis around Raipur. Had the Chinnaswamy data been applied, the entire analytical frame would have been wrong. This is the fourth consecutive experiment where venue data hygiene was correctly handled, and the most consequential instance — the pipeline's discrepancy detection is now a proven strength.

The scenario that activated — RCB winning the toss and bowling first — was precisely the one the memo identified as pushing toward the upper band. The conditional estimate of ~59% RCB was above the market's 57.5% and proved correct. This demonstrates that the memo's unconditional midpoint of 54% was not "wrong" — it was an appropriate pre-toss weighted average. The conditional framework added genuine analytical value beyond a point estimate.

### What the memo got wrong

- **The Raipur pitch characterization was significantly wrong, and the memo was too anchored on the 7-match venue average.** The memo described the pitch as "black-soil, slow and low, inconsistent bounce" with "par score 149-165" and "average 1st innings score ~147-149." KKR scored 192/4 — the highest team total in Raipur IPL history, 27+ runs above the memo's upper par estimate and 43+ runs above the cited average. RCB then chased 193 in 19.1 overs. The match produced 386 runs in 39.1 overs. The memo acknowledged the Source Quality Clerk's warning that the 7-match sample had "huge confidence intervals" and the pitch report lacked a curator quote. But the reasoning still treated the ~149 average as directionally informative. The critical error was not recognizing that 5 of Raipur's 7 IPL matches were from 2014-2015 — a fundamentally different era of T20 batting. The 2026 IPL features different ball characteristics, deeper batting lineups, and evolved power-hitting. Applying a 2014-2015 scoring norm to a 2026 match is analogous to applying Chinnaswamy 2015 data to Chinnaswamy 2026 — the venue is the same but the run-scoring environment has shifted dramatically. The memo's own evidence (RCB's 167/8 three days earlier, itself a Raipur record) hinted that the historical average was stale.

- **The spin matchup analysis consumed significant analytical space but was rendered largely irrelevant by selection.** The memo invested considerable reasoning in the spin battle: Narine (eco 6.83) and Varun (eco 8.67) vs Suyash Sharma (eco 8.31) and Krunal Pandya (eco 8.54). Reasoning point #4 was dedicated to this. In the event: Varun didn't play, Suyash Sharma wasn't even selected (replaced by Jacob Duffy, a pacer), and the pitch played much faster than "spin-friendly." Narine bowled 1/31 — competent but not the match-defining performance the analysis anticipated. The analytical focus was misallocated because it relied on speculative playing XIs that the Source Quality Clerk had explicitly flagged as "speculative with known disagreements." When XIs are unconfirmed, building detailed tactical matchups around predicted selections creates analytical fragility — the reasoning evaporates if the predictions are wrong.

- **Kohli's recent form was framed as a concern rather than modeled as high-variance.** The memo noted "back-to-back ducks in recent matches despite leading RCB's season batting aggregate (379 runs, SR 156.6)." The implicit framing was bearish on Kohli. He scored 105*(60) — his ninth IPL century, the match-winning innings, and one of the best individual performances of the tournament. The season aggregate (379 runs, SR 156.6) was the more informative prior; the back-to-back ducks were two data points from a high-variance distribution. Entry 4 (include all-rounders in batting depth) was applied to Krunal, but no equivalent framework existed for handling short-run form noise in established batters with strong season numbers. The memo would have been better served by noting the high variance in Kohli's recent outputs (ducks AND centuries are in the distribution) rather than framing the ducks as a directional concern.

- **The XI predictions were partially wrong, and the analytical consequences were underestimated.** The actual RCB XI included Venkatesh Iyer and Jacob Duffy — neither in the predicted XI. Suyash Sharma (predicted) was dropped. The actual KKR XI started Finn Allen (which the memo flagged as uncertain) and dropped Varun (which the memo rated as "likely to push through"). These differences were material: Duffy replacing Suyash changes RCB's bowling composition from spin-heavy to pace-heavy; Venkatesh Iyer adds batting depth. The Source Quality Clerk's warning about speculative XIs was sound but insufficiently internalized in the reasoning — the memo built specific matchup arguments around a predicted XI that didn't materialize.

- **The attribution of the market's 3.5pp premium to "anchoring and name recognition" was too dismissive.** The memo said the market's 57.5% was explainable by "anchoring to RCB's table position and name recognition (Kohli, Patidar) rather than by factors our base-rate construction missed." But RCB's table position (1st/2nd, 14 pts) was a genuine strength — this match confirmed it by pushing them to the top. Kohli's "name recognition" wasn't empty — his century demonstrated that the market's implicit weighting of his quality was, if anything, accurate. The memo's base-rate construction captured standings quality (+4-6pp) and Bhuvneshwar (+2pp) but may have underweighted RCB's overall squad depth and Kohli's match-winning ceiling. When the market's premium is attributable to verifiable strengths rather than cognitive biases, the Entry 8 correction should be smaller.

### What was missed

- **The era gap in venue statistics was not identified as a systematic bias.** Raipur's 7 IPL matches span 2014-2015 (5 matches) and 2026 (2 matches). The 2026 matches (RCB 167/8 vs MI, and now KKR 192/4) averaged ~180, while the 2014-15 matches averaged ~140. Using the blended average (~149) as "par score" systematically underestimates run-scoring in 2026 conditions. T20 cricket's run-scoring evolution from 2014 to 2026 is well-documented and should have been treated as a confound when using old venue data. The memo could have noted that the 2026 Raipur matches were tracking 30-40 runs above the historical average and adjusted accordingly.

- **Bhuvneshwar's powerplay deployment against Allen was a predictable matchup that deserved modeling.** The memo identified Bhuvneshwar as the strongest individual signal and Allen's starting role as a key uncertainty. But it didn't connect the dots: if Allen starts, Bhuvneshwar's new-ball skill against an aggressive opener becomes a specific, high-value matchup. In the event, Bhuvneshwar dismissed Allen (18 off 8) in the powerplay, exactly as this compound matchup would predict. This is an Entry 2 (compound bowling quality) application that was available but not articulated.

- **The impact sub asymmetry between the teams was not fully modeled.** RCB used Padikkal as impact sub — he scored 39 and put on a 92-run partnership with Kohli that was the backbone of the chase. KKR used Saurabh Dubey (0/21) — a negligible contribution. The memo listed Padikkal as RCB's impact sub option and Finn Allen as KKR's. But Allen was in the starting XI, meaning KKR's impact sub pool was weakened (Dubey instead of Allen). The net impact sub advantage shifted materially toward RCB once Allen started, and this was not captured. Entry 3's application note about modeling replacements reciprocally extends to impact sub deployment.

### Decisive Factor

The match was decided by Virat Kohli's unbeaten 105 off 60 balls (11 fours, 3 sixes, SR 175), supported by a 92-run second-wicket partnership with impact sub Devdutt Padikkal (39). KKR posted a competitive 192/4 — driven by Raghuvanshi's 71(46) and Rinku's 49*(25) — but RCB's chase never faltered. Kohli reached his fifty in 32 balls and accelerated through the back end, with RCB at 157/3 after 16 overs before Jitesh Sharma hit the winning boundary with 5 balls to spare. Kartik Tyagi (3/32) was KKR's best bowler, but Narine (1/31) couldn't apply the spin stranglehold the Raipur narrative predicted, especially with Varun absent. Bhuvneshwar dismissed Allen in the powerplay (18 off 8, c Jitesh b Bhuvneshwar), limiting KKR's explosive start.

The memo's core framework — RCB modestly favored, with the toss as the largest swing factor — was correct. The specific mechanism (Kohli century in the chase) was not modeled as a scenario, though the memo's batting depth analysis named Kohli as RCB's leading run-scorer. The conditional band for "RCB wins toss and bowls first" (56-62%) captured the result. The memo would have scored higher if it had (a) recognized the era gap in Raipur's scoring norms, (b) avoided building detailed matchups around unconfirmed XIs, and (c) modeled Kohli's upside as a match-winning scenario rather than framing his form as a concern.

## Lessons for Reflection Log

- **When a venue's match sample spans multiple IPL eras (e.g., 2014-2015 and 2026), segment the scoring norms by era rather than blending them into a single average.** Raipur's blended 7-match average was ~149. The 2014-2015 subset averaged ~140; the 2026 subset averaged ~180. KKR scored 192/4. T20 batting has evolved dramatically — ball composition, power-hitting depth, and strategic intent have all shifted upward. A blended average that weights 2014-2015 equally with 2026 systematically underestimates modern run-scoring at the venue. When most of a venue's sample is from a prior era, the recent-era subset (even if 1-2 matches) is more informative than the blended figure.

- **When playing XIs are unconfirmed pre-toss, do not build detailed tactical matchups (bowler X vs batter Y, spin attack comparison) around predicted XIs — instead model overall team quality and note that specific matchups are contingent on selection.** The memo built reasoning around Suyash Sharma vs Narine/Varun. None played against each other: Suyash was dropped, Varun was injured out. Analytical space spent on speculative matchups creates fragile reasoning that collapses if selections differ. Reserve detailed matchup analysis for post-toss memos with confirmed XIs.
