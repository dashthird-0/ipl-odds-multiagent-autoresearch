# Post-Match Grade: Lucknow Super Giants vs Chennai Super Kings
## 2026-05-15, BRSABV Ekana Cricket Stadium, Lucknow

**Result:** Lucknow Super Giants won by 7 wickets (20 balls remaining). LSG 188/3 in 16.4 overs chasing CSK 187/5 in 20 overs. Mitchell Marsh 90(38), Kartik Sharma 71(42), Pooran 32*(17), Inglis 36(32), Dube 32*(16). Akash Singh 3/26, Shahbaz Ahmed 1/45. LSG won toss and fielded. Kamboj 0/63 in 2.4 overs (economy 23.63). Man of the Match: Mitchell Marsh.
**Memo directional view:** CSK marginally favored. Best estimate CSK 50-55%, LSG 45-50%. Midpoint ~50% CSK.
**Band:** CSK 45-55% / LSG 45-55% — Result **within band.** LSG won at an implied 45-50% pre-match probability. The band, deliberately wide (plus/minus 8pp) and centered near 50%, captured the outcome. The directional lean toward CSK was marginal (2.5pp at most) and wrong, but the band's honest acknowledgment of deep uncertainty — no market anchor, no venue data — meant the miss was contained. This is the seventh band coverage in eight experiments.

## Reasoning Grade: B

### What the memo got right

- **The market price was correctly discarded — the single most important analytical call in this experiment.** The Polymarket implied probability of 84.9% LSG was backed by $52.83 in volume. The memo (and Skeptic) identified this as "not a market; it is approximately one person's bet" and discarded it entirely, treating the match as having no market anchor. Had the memo anchored on 84.9% LSG and adjusted even partially toward it, the band would have been centered above 60% LSG and the reasoning would have been contaminated by noise. By discarding the non-functional market, the memo freed itself to build a probability estimate purely from analytical foundations — H2H (3-2 LSG), form (CSK superior), venue record (LSG 0-3 at Ekana), and personnel changes (Overton out). The resulting near-50% estimate was far more accurate than the market's 85%. This is the strongest case in the experiment series of a market price being pure noise rather than a signal to lean away from.

- **Entry 7 (dead-rubber teams not systematically weaker) received its fourth consecutive validation.** LSG — mathematically eliminated, on a 2W-6L run, with their captain reportedly "on the firing line" — won comprehensively by 7 wickets with 20 balls to spare. The memo applied 0pp for dead-rubber motivation, citing three prior validations (Exp 4: DC won with 5 changes; Exp 5: GT won; Exp 7: MI won missing captain and vice-captain). Mitchell Marsh (90 off 38, SR 236.84) produced his best performance of the season in a dead-rubber match. Players fighting for contracts and reputation in meaningless matches can be more dangerous than pressured playoff-hunting opponents. Entry 7 is now the most consistently validated rule in the reflection log.

- **Entry 1 (discount contradicted home advantage) was correctly applied — LSG received 0pp home advantage.** LSG's 2026 Ekana record in clear results was 0W-3L entering this match. The memo correctly applied Entry 1 to assign zero home advantage, noting: "Here the contradiction is total — LSG's Ekana record is worse than neutral." LSG won, making their clear-result record 1W-3L, but this does not invalidate the 0pp call. A team winning at ~45-50% probability at their home ground is consistent with no home advantage — if they had a genuine home advantage, the probability would have been higher. The framework's job is to estimate the probability correctly, not to predict the binary outcome, and a 45-50% win probability that produces a win is well-calibrated.

- **The wide band was justified and appropriate.** The memo identified three compounding reasons for a plus/minus 8pp band: (1) no market anchor, (2) venue stats engine returned zero matches due to a name mismatch (Entry 13 pattern), (3) dead-rubber behavioral uncertainty. The Skeptic concurred. This was the correct response to genuine, unresolvable uncertainty — honest rather than artificially precise. A narrower band (e.g., plus/minus 5pp centered at 52% CSK) would have barely covered the result and created false confidence. The wide band acknowledged that the memo was operating with less calibration data than any previous experiment.

- **Overton's absence was correctly identified as the main uncertainty and directionally accurate.** The memo stated: "This is the single factor most likely to swing the outcome in either direction." It assessed a 3-5pp adjustment toward LSG for Overton's loss, noting CSK retained Kamboj, Noor Ahmad, and Hosein. CSK posted 187/5 — within the pre-match par expectation of 165-175 for Ekana — and their bowling was demolished in the chase (188/3 conceded in 16.4 overs, including Kamboj's 0/63 at economy 23.63). The Overton absence weakened CSK's bowling more than the 3-5pp estimate captured, but the direction was correct and it was the memo's primary hedge against CSK.

- **Entry 5 (confirmed XI means injury resolved) was correctly applied for Mohsin Khan and Inglis.** Both were flagged as returning from niggles that ruled them out of the May 7 match. The memo applied Entry 5: "once in the confirmed XI, discount injury-based performance concerns by at least 75%." Inglis scored 36(32) opening the batting and shared a 135-run partnership with Marsh. The entry continues to hold — confirmed selection implies medical clearance.

- **The toss adjustment was appropriately capped per Entry 9.** With low dew (dewpoints 20-23C, humidity 42-66%, temperatures above 30C) and a tiny venue sample (3-of-4 chasing wins in 2026), the memo capped the toss at +2-3pp for LSG. The toss winner won, but the match was decided by Marsh's powerplay carnage (86/0 in 6 overs) rather than by second-innings dew advantage. Capping the toss was the correct methodology even though the toss winner won.

- **The venue identification was correct, preventing a repeat of Exp 7's catastrophic error.** The match was at Ekana, Lucknow — correctly stated throughout the memo. The stats engine name mismatch (returning zero venue matches) was transparently acknowledged rather than ignored, and the memo adapted by sourcing venue data from the news analyst's five 2026 match summaries. This proactive acknowledgment of the data gap, though not a full fix, prevented the pipeline from operating on wrong data as in Exp 7.

### What the memo got wrong

- **The directional lean toward CSK was wrong, and the margin of victory was larger than any scenario the memo entertained.** The memo called CSK "marginally favored" at 50-55%. LSG won by 7 wickets with 20 balls to spare — a comprehensive result, not a coin-flip margin. While a 45-50% team winning is not unusual in any single instance, the *manner* of the win (188/3 in 16.4 overs chasing 187) suggests LSG's actual probability was higher than 45-50%. The CSK form advantage (5W-3L vs 2W-6L in last 8, 3-match winning streak) that drove the lean proved uninformative when LSG's individual ceiling performances materialized and CSK's bowling collapsed without Overton.

- **The Overton adjustment of 3-5pp was insufficient — CSK's bowling unit collapsed, not just the replacement slot.** The memo correctly identified Overton's absence as the main uncertainty but focused narrowly on the quality gap between Overton and his replacement (Prashant Veer). In the event, Prashant Veer was adequate (13* batting, didn't bowl disastrously). The actual bowling collapse came from Kamboj — the bowler the memo cited as proof CSK "retained" bowling strength — who conceded 0/63 in 2.4 overs (economy 23.63). This reveals a blind spot: when a key bowler is absent, the cascading effect on role assignments can expose other bowlers in unfamiliar phases. Kamboj may have been forced into death-overs responsibility he normally shares with Overton. The memo's Entry 3 framework ("model the replacement XI quality") was correctly applied but too narrowly scoped — it should extend to how the absence reorganizes the entire bowling plan, not just the one-for-one swap.

- **CSK's top-order failure was not modeled as a scenario.** CSK's top 3 — Samson (20 off 20, SR 100), Gaikwad (13 off 9), Urvil Patel (6 off 7) — combined for 39 off 36 balls before all three fell to Akash Singh. The memo's batting comparison (Skeptic Challenge 8: "CSK batting marginally superior, +2-3pp") was based on aggregate stats: Samson 402 runs SR 163.4, Gaikwad 251 runs. When CSK's top 3 collectively underperformed, the batting advantage evaporated. The memo's "What Would Change" section should have included a scenario for CSK batting failure: "If CSK's top order fails (sub-50 in the powerplay), the form advantage disappears and the match reverts to a near-random contest." Samson scoring at SR 100 vs his season 163.4 is rare but not unforeseeable, especially against quality pace bowling.

- **Marsh's explosive ceiling was present in the data but unweighted.** Marsh entered with 367 runs at SR 148.0 — solid but not explosive. His 90(38) at SR 236.84 was far above his season average. The memo (following Entry 12) assessed "team-level quality" rather than modeling individual ceiling performances, which was methodologically correct for pre-toss analysis. However, the stats snapshot showed Marsh as LSG's clear number-1 batter with 367 runs — 130 runs ahead of Pant. When one player carries that much of a team's run production and has the demonstrated ability to bat at 148+ SR, his ceiling day represents a significant probability mass in LSG's distribution.

### What was missed

- **The impact substitution brought in a match-changing bowler who was not in the starting XI.** Akash Singh — not named in the confirmed playing XI of 11 — bowled 4-0-26-3, dismissing Gaikwad (13), Samson (20), and Urvil Patel (6). His three-wicket haul reduced CSK from 31/1 to 52/3, collapsing the top order and suppressing the total to 187. The memo's analysis was based on the confirmed bowling attack (Shami, Prince Yadav, Mohsin Khan, Shahbaz Ahmed, Digvesh Rathi). A player outside this XI taking 3 wickets at economy 6.50 is an unmodeled upside scenario. The memo mentioned impact subs as an uncertainty but did not model how LSG might use the rule to strengthen their bowling — despite Entry 3 specifically calling for replacement XI assessment in both directions.

- **CSK also used impact sub changes that altered their bowling composition.** Spencer Johnson bowled (4-0-39-1) and Gurjapneet Singh bowled (3-0-34-0) in the chase, yet neither was in the confirmed CSK XI. The memo analyzed CSK's bowling as Kamboj/Noor Ahmad/Hosein/Mukesh Choudhary/Prashant Veer. The actual attack was materially different. Impact sub decisions are made during the match and cannot be predicted pre-toss, but the memo should acknowledge the magnitude of uncertainty they create: the bowling attack that takes the field may differ from the confirmed XI by one or two bowlers.

- **The 135-run opening partnership between Marsh and Inglis destroyed the chase within the powerplay.** LSG scored 86/0 in the first 6 overs — already halfway to the target with all 10 wickets in hand. The 5th over bowled by Kamboj went for 28 runs (four consecutive Marsh sixes plus a four). This hyperaggressive powerplay approach is a distinctive feature of T20 chases where the batting team has nothing to lose — consistent with Entry 7's finding that dead-rubber teams can play with unusual freedom. The memo noted LSG's "nothing to lose" status but didn't connect it to the possibility of tactically aggressive batting approaches that dead-rubber liberation enables.

- **The memo's "What Would Change" scenarios focused on first-innings outcomes but not on chase dynamics.** The three scenarios listed were: first-innings score above 185, Prashant Veer's early performance, and rain interruption. None modeled the chase. CSK posted 187 — just above the 185 threshold — which the memo predicted would "shift the balance toward the bowling team." Instead, LSG chased 188 in 16.4 overs. The 185 threshold was calibrated for Ekana's typically lower-scoring conditions but did not account for the possibility that an explosive chase could make even 187 look below-par. A conditional like "If LSG score 80+ in the powerplay, the match is effectively over regardless of total" would have better captured the chase-phase dynamics.

### Decisive Factor

Mitchell Marsh's 90 off 38 balls (9 fours, 7 sixes, SR 236.84) was the decisive performance. Marsh and Inglis put on 135 for the first wicket in 11.4 overs, reducing a 188-run chase to a formality by the end of the powerplay. The critical moment was the 5th over bowled by Anshul Kamboj: Marsh struck four consecutive sixes and a four for 28 runs from the over. At 86/0 after 6 overs, LSG needed only 102 from 84 balls with all wickets in hand. Even when three wickets fell in a brief wobble at 135-144, Nicholas Pooran (32* off 17, including four consecutive sixes off Kamboj in the 17th over) sealed the match with 20 balls to spare.

CSK's first innings was undermined by Akash Singh's 3/26, which dismissed the entire top 3 (Gaikwad 13, Samson 20, Urvil Patel 6). From 52/3, Kartik Sharma's career-best 71(42) rescued CSK to a competitive 187/5, aided by Shivam Dube's 32*(16) in the death overs. But 187 proved wholly inadequate against Marsh's onslaught.

The bowling analysis is revealing: Kamboj, cited by the memo as proof CSK "retained" bowling quality without Overton, conceded 0/63 in 2.4 overs — economy 23.63, the worst individual bowling figures of the match and possibly the season. Noor Ahmad (4-0-21-0) was economical but wicketless. Spencer Johnson (4-0-39-1) took a wicket but was expensive. CSK's bowling was not weakened incrementally by Overton's absence — it was exposed catastrophically when Kamboj was targeted by power hitters operating without pressure.

The dead-rubber dynamic was the invisible accelerant. Marsh's 90 off 38 — his best performance of the season, on a pitch averaging 164-175 par, in a meaningless match — epitomizes Entry 7's core finding: eliminated teams are unpredictable, not systematically weaker. A team with nothing to lose can produce explosive ceiling performances that playoff-hunting opponents, burdened by consequence, cannot match. CSK's "must-win" pressure and 3-match winning streak counted for nothing against an opponent swinging freely.

## Lessons for Reflection Log

- **Entry 7 (dead-rubber teams not systematically weaker) now has four consecutive validations (Exp 4, 5, 7, 8) and zero counter-examples in the experiment series.** Upgrade from "tentative" to "validated." At this point the rule should be applied with high confidence. Dead-rubber adjustments of any size should be treated as a cataloged bias — the pipeline's default should be 0pp with no exceptions unless a player or team explicitly signals reduced effort through a confirmed source.

- **When a key bowler is absent, model the cascading effect on the remaining bowling unit's role assignments, not just the one-for-one replacement quality.** Overton's absence was assessed as a 3-5pp adjustment based on Prashant Veer's quality relative to Overton. But the actual bowling collapse came from Kamboj (0/63), an existing bowler who was exposed in a role he may have been shielded from when Overton shared the workload. Entry 3's framework ("model the replacement XI quality") should extend to assessing which OTHER bowlers are pushed into unfamiliar phases or higher-pressure roles by the absence. The one-for-one swap understates the true impact of losing a key bowler who anchored the unit's structure.

- **Impact substitutions can introduce match-changing performers not present in the confirmed XI.** Akash Singh (3/26) was not in LSG's confirmed starting XI but delivered the most impactful bowling performance of the match. The impact sub rule allows teams to introduce specialists tailored to match conditions — a fast bowler for powerplay swing, a spin option for middle overs, or an explosive batter for death overs. The memo's pre-match analysis was locked to the confirmed XI and did not model impact sub possibilities. While individual impact sub choices are unpredictable, the RANGE of possible bowling/batting compositions is wider than the starting XI implies, and this should widen the band or temper confidence in matchup-specific analysis.
