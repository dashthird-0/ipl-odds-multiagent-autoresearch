### What the memo got wrong

- **The directional lean toward GT was wrong, and KKR's margin of victory (29 runs) reflected genuine superiority on the day, not coin-flip variance.** The memo's midpoint was GT 57% / KKR 43%. KKR posted 247/2 -- a total that renders most structural analysis irrelevant. The memo was not wrong to favor GT on the evidence, but the implied probability of 43% for KKR may have been 3-5pp too low given KKR's recent form trajectory (discussed below). A midpoint closer to GT 52-54% would have been more accurate and would have better aligned with the market (55.5% GT). The 3pp gap between the Skeptic's composite and the market was explainable but should have raised the question: is the model finding a real edge, or overfitting to GT's headline form?

- **The form analysis overweighted KKR's 2W-6L headline record while underweighting their recent 4-match winning streak.** The stats_snapshot showed KKR 2W-6L in their last 8 completed matches. But the news_conditions report revealed a critical trajectory: KKR had won 4 consecutive matches before losing to RCB on May 13 -- and that RCB loss came against Kohli's 105(60), a near-unplayable innings. The Skeptic noted KKR were "not on a current losing streak" and Entry 14 did not trigger, but this observation was framed as a negative finding (no additional downward pressure) rather than a positive one (KKR's quality had improved mid-season). The 2W-6L figure was heavily influenced by early-season losses (SRH by 65 runs on April 2, LSG by 3 wickets on April 9) that no longer reflected the team's mid-May quality. The pipeline detects negative momentum (Entry 14: extended losing streaks) but has no equivalent mechanism for detecting positive momentum reversals within an otherwise poor-looking record.

- **GT's bowling superiority was real but its magnitude was overstated relative to the specific conditions.** Key Reasoning Point 2 described GT's bowling as having "clear superiority in depth and economy" with Rabada, Siraj, Rashid Khan, and Holder as "four high-quality options." On the day: Rashid Khan 0/57 (economy 14.25), Siraj 1/50 (economy 12.50), Rabada 0/40 (economy 10.00), Holder 0/39 (economy 9.75). Every single one of GT's "high-quality" bowlers was expensive. The aggregate season stats accurately described central tendency but failed to capture the distribution's lower tail -- the possibility that all four bowlers could simultaneously fail against explosive batting on a flat track. The Skeptic rated this claim as "supported" -- the weakest single assessment in the review, given how fragile bowling stats prove to be on batting-friendly surfaces.

- **The scenario threshold of 185+ for KKR's first innings was far too low for the actual conditions.** The memo stated "If KKR post 185+ in the first innings, a high total on a dewy night compresses GT's structural advantages." KKR posted 247 -- 62 runs beyond the threshold. If AllCric's "190+ average first-innings score in IPL 2026" at Eden Gardens had any validity (the Source Quality Clerk was rightly skeptical), then 185 is below-par -- a below-par score should not be framed as a scenario that "compresses advantages." The memo distrusted both competing data points (178 stale, 190+ speculative) but calibrated its threshold closer to the stale figure. A "what would change" threshold of 200+ would have been more appropriate for a surface where the highest total was 226/8 and the recent match average was purportedly 190+.

### What was missed

- **Finn Allen was the match winner, but he was invisible in the analytical frame.** Allen scored 93 off 35 balls with 10 sixes -- the most destructive innings by any KKR batter this season, and the fourth-ever IPL career with multiple 10-six innings. Yet Allen did not appear in the stats_snapshot's top 5 batters for KKR (Raghuvanshi 268, RK Singh 241, Rahane 205, Green 199, Powell 121). He was in the confirmed XI and was noted in the news_conditions as "retained over Tim Seifert" -- meaning he had been dropped at some point, suppressing his aggregate stats. The memo analyzed batting depth using season aggregates that excluded the match's most dangerous batter. This echoes the Exp 8 lesson about Marsh (season SR 148.0 obscured match-day ceiling of 236.84) but is more severe: Allen was not even IN the aggregates. When a recently returned player with known explosive ability is confirmed in the XI, their ceiling is a band-widening factor that season stats systematically underrepresent.

- **Pathirana's team affiliation was wrong in the pre-match intelligence.** The news_conditions report stated "Matheesha Pathirana (GT) unavailable. Has received NOC from Sri Lanka Cricket but not available for this game due to logistical reasons." The Source Quality Clerk correctly rated this as "speculative" and "effectively hearsay." In fact, Pathirana was a KKR player and was their impact player choice. This was either a mid-season acquisition or a factual error in the news intelligence. The Source Quality Clerk's skepticism was validated, but the error of team affiliation -- a verifiable fact -- was not caught by any agent. The error was not consequential (Pathirana bowled only 1.2 overs before a hamstring injury), but it reveals a gap in fact-checking: player team affiliation is deterministic and verifiable from squad registration data, yet the pipeline treated a speculative claim about it as merely low-confidence rather than verifying it against the confirmed XIs (which listed Pathirana among KKR's impact player nominations).

- **Sai Sudharsan's retirement hurt at 23*(13) in the 3rd over was a mid-innings disruption that fundamentally reshaped the chase.** Struck on the left elbow by Kartik Tyagi, GT's leading run-scorer (385 runs, SR 154.0) was absent for 14 overs during the most critical phase of the chase. He returned in the 17th over and finished 53*(28), but the middle-order was exposed earlier than planned (Sindhu 1(4)). In-match injuries are not predictable, but the CONSEQUENCE of losing a specific player mid-innings is an analytically useful pre-match scenario. The memo's scenario analysis focused on first-innings outcomes and Varun's fitness, not on the fragility of GT's chase if their star batter was disrupted. GT's top-order depth (Gill, Sudharsan, Buttler all 149+ SR) was the memo's strongest argument for their chasing quality -- but depth becomes less relevant when the best batter is absent for 70% of the innings.

- **KKR's tactical use of the impact player rule to bring in Pathirana was a sophisticated move the pre-match intelligence entirely missed.** The starting XI had only 4 frontline bowlers (Narine, Varun, Tyagi, Dubey) plus the part-time options (Green, Anukul Roy). Adding Pathirana -- an international-class death-overs specialist -- via impact sub expanded the effective bowling resources without sacrificing batting depth. Entry 16 warns about this pattern abstractly, but the specific tactical implication -- that a bowler-light XI can be deceptive about the team's true bowling depth -- was not explored. When Pathirana was injured after 1.2 overs, KKR had to defend 247 with effectively 5 bowling options (Narine, Varun, Tyagi, Green, Dubey plus Anukul Roy), and they still succeeded.

### Decisive Factor

Finn Allen's 93 off 35 balls (4 fours, 10 sixes, SR 265.71) was the match-defining innings. Allen attacked from ball one, reaching his fifty in just 21 balls -- off a Rashid Khan over -- and became only the fourth batter in IPL history with multiple innings of 10+ sixes in a career. After Rahane fell at 44-1 in the 4.3rd over, Allen put on 95 with Raghuvanshi, and by the time he was caught off Sai Kishore at 139-2 in the 11.2nd over, the match was already shaped in KKR's favor.

Raghuvanshi (82* off 44) and Green (52* off 28) then added an unbeaten 108 for the third wicket in 8.4 overs to push KKR to 247/2 -- the highest total between these two sides and a target that GT's elite batting could not overhaul despite excellent individual efforts.

GT's chase was immediately disrupted when Sudharsan retired hurt at 23*(13) in the 3rd over after being struck on the elbow by Kartik Tyagi. Despite this setback, Gill (85 off 49, SR 173.47) and Buttler (57 off 35, SR 162.86) mounted a genuine challenge -- GT were 177/2 in the 16th over, needing 70 from 22 balls with 8 wickets in hand. But Narine's breakthrough (Gill caught in the 17th over) and Saurabh Dubey's exceptional 19th over (5 runs, Buttler dismissed) choked the late chase. GT finished 218/4 -- an excellent total on any other night, but 29 runs short of the extraordinary target.

The toss, won by GT, gave them the preferred second-innings position with heavy dew (humidity 88-90% in the second innings). But 247 was simply too high. Entry 6's symmetry principle held perfectly: flat conditions helped both innings (247 AND 218 are exceptional totals), but the MAGNITUDE of the first-innings exploitation exceeded what the second innings could match even under optimal conditions. The toss was not the decisive factor -- the batting-first total was so large that dew could not compensate for scoreboard pressure.

The dead-rubber narrative was the match's invisible tailwind for the fifth consecutive experiment. Allen's freedom -- attacking Rashid Khan from the start, clearing the ropes 10 times without apparent consequence-management -- mirrors the pattern seen with Miller (Exp 4), MI (Exp 7), and Marsh (Exp 8). Dead-rubber teams play with liberation, and that liberation can produce the highest-ceiling performances of the tournament. The market prices these teams lower because their season record is poor; but season record reflects average quality, not match-day ceiling, and dead-rubber teams face no downside to swinging for that ceiling.

The memo's analytical framework was sound in structure: it correctly identified the first-innings total as the swing variable, applied six reflection log entries appropriately, aligned with a functioning market, and justified a standard-to-wide band. The directional miss (GT favored at 57%, KKR won by 29 runs) was driven by two factors: underweighting KKR's recent form trajectory (4-match winning streak hidden by a 2W-6L headline), and underestimating the explosive upside of Allen -- a player not in the season's top-5 stats due to limited matches. The band captured the result (KKR 37-49%, KKR won). This is the pipeline operating within its design tolerance: wrong on direction, right on range, transparent about its reasoning.
