### What the memo got wrong

- **The memo produced no independent directional view — it anchored to the market and stayed there.** The midpoint (44% DC / 56% RR) was within 0.5pp of the market anchor (43.5% DC / 56.5% RR). The memo explicitly stated: "No confident directional divergence from the market is warranted." DC won. This is the fifth experiment (after Exp 4, 5, 7, 10) where leaning away from the market would have been correct, and the first where the memo failed to lean at all. Entry 8 (lean toward base-rate estimate when divergence is analytically explained) was NOT triggered because the gap was <3pp — but the analytical basis for a DC lean existed. The market was wrong; the memo agreed with the market; the memo was therefore wrong by agreement rather than independent analysis.

- **Entry 1 was over-applied: the opponent-specific H2H signal was suppressed by the team-level home record.** DC's 6-3 record vs RR at Arun Jaitley was discounted because DC's overall 2026 home record was 1-6. But the 1-6 record was against SIX DIFFERENT opponents — it captured DC's general home struggles, not their matchup against RR specifically. The venue H2H (6-3 vs RR, now 7-3) captures something persistent about how DC match up against RR at this ground: perhaps RR's batting style is vulnerable to DC's pace attack at this venue, or DC's batters find confidence against RR's bowling. The memo treated DC's home record and venue H2H as competing signals where the home record dominated. In reality, the opponent-specific signal (H2H) and the general signal (home record) answer different questions — "how does DC perform at home against everyone?" vs "how does DC perform at home against THIS team?" When these diverge, the opponent-specific signal should not be fully overridden by the general signal.

- **The form trajectory uplift (Entry 19) was identified but not converted into a directional lean.** The Skeptic recommended 2-3pp uplift for DC's improving form. The memo accepted this but concluded the uplift merely aligned the estimate with the market rather than pushing beyond it. The reasoning chain was: market says 43.5% DC; base rate analysis (with home record penalty) suggests ~42-46% DC; Entry 19 provides 2-3pp uplift; final estimate ~44% DC — essentially the market price. The error was treating Entry 19's uplift as bringing the estimate UP TO the market rather than ABOVE it. If the form trajectory is real (it was), and the market is not pricing it (unclear), and the base-rate estimate without the trajectory is BELOW the market (which the Skeptic's 42-46% range suggests), then the trajectory should push the estimate ABOVE the market. The memo collapsed these steps into market agreement.

- **The RR bowling quality advantage was overstated relative to what materialized at this venue.** The memo identified Archer (15 wickets) as the elite threat and DC's non-Starc bowling as "porous." In the match: Archer bowled 2/35 — good but not match-changing. Adam Milne (RR's other overseas pacer) conceded 0/41 in 3.2 overs — the most expensive spell of the match. Brijesh Sharma (2/44) was adequate. RR's bowling collectively couldn't defend 193. Meanwhile, DC's "porous" bowling attack delivered: Starc 4/40, Ngidi 2/24, Madhav Tiwari 2/27. The Skeptic's Challenge 7 explicitly warned that "the bowling quality gap narrows on a batting-friendly surface" and "RR's non-Archer bowlers (Burger 10.45 econ) are similarly vulnerable." This was precisely correct. The memo accepted the challenge ("on net, RR's bowling attack is materially stronger") when the correct conclusion was "the gap is smaller than season aggregates suggest because venue conditions compress bowling effectiveness."

- **DC winning the toss was not adequately explored as a scenario.** The memo listed "if DC win the toss and bat first" as a +3-5pp uplift scenario. DC won the toss and elected to BOWL — not bat. The bat-first win rate at Arun Jaitley is 56.2%, and DC chose against this trend. The memo didn't model the scenario "DC win toss and choose to bowl" — which might reflect confidence in their death bowling or specific matchup intelligence. Since DC won despite choosing against the venue trend, the toss itself was perhaps +1-2pp (toss winner match winner is 50.5% at this venue) — minor, consistent with Entry 9's cap.

### What was missed

Entry 18 (weight international profile over season stats for ceiling assessment) applies to bowlers as well as batters: Starc's career T20I and IPL pedigree (143 T20I wickets, multiple IPL spells of 3+/match) indicates a ceiling far above "DC's bowling is porous." When one bowler can single-handedly change a match in one over, the team's bowling vulnerability is partially offset by that bowler's explosion probability. ### Decisive Factor

Mitchell Starc's death-overs spell was the match's pivot. RR were 161/2 after 14.1 overs with Parag (51 off 26, 5 sixes) and Jurel (53 off 40) having built a formidable platform. RR appeared headed for 220+ on a ground where that total would have been extremely difficult to chase. Then Starc produced one of the great IPL death-overs spells: 3 wickets in 4 balls, dismissing Parag (caught, ending a destructive 51), Donovan Ferreira (golden duck, first ball), and another batter in quick succession. The collapse was total: 6 wickets fell for 32 runs in the final 5.5 overs, reducing RR from a dominant 161/2 to a below-par 193/8.

The collapse converted the match from "RR need to defend 220+" (where their bowling, led by Archer, would likely have been sufficient) to "RR need to defend 193" (where DC's batting depth, particularly Rahul's form, made a successful chase probable). Starc's spell didn't just take wickets — it fundamentally altered the match's probability landscape in a single over.

On the chase side, DC's 105-run opening partnership between Porel (51 off 31) and KL Rahul (56 off 42) meant the target was never in doubt. Rahul's innings — his 9th fifty-plus score of the season at the venue where he'd made 152* earlier — demonstrated why the Skeptic's Anchoring Check identified his form as the key underweighted factor. Axar Patel's 34*(18) and Ashutosh Sharma's explosive 18*(5) finished the job with 4 balls to spare.

The match validated several analytical threads:
1. **Starc's individual ceiling** — a world-class bowler can negate a team's overall bowling weakness in a single spell. The memo framed DC's bowling around its weaknesses (Kuldeep, Mukesh), but Starc's ceiling was the relevant signal in a format where one great over can decide the match.
2. **KL Rahul's venue mastery** — 445 runs at SR 172.5 in the season, including 152* at this ground. His 56(42) was characteristically controlled rather than explosive, but the consistency was the point: he doesn't fail here.
3. **DC's tactical evolution** — Porel's promotion (replacing Nissanka) gave DC a second aggressive opener. The 105-run stand (at 6.2 RPO in the powerplay and beyond) mirrored the successful May 11 chase (also with Porel opening). Entry 19's form trajectory is partly about wins/losses and partly about tactical adaptations that compound.
4. **Venue-specific H2H persistence** — DC's 7-3 record vs RR at Arun Jaitley suggests a genuine persistent dynamic. Whether it's DC's pace attack exploiting RR's batting approach, or DC's batters finding confidence against RR's bowling, the signal is now 7-3 across multiple years and squad compositions.

## Lessons for Reflection Log

- **Refine Entry 1 — distinguish opponent-specific H2H from general home record.** DC's 1-6 home record in 2026 was against six different opponents. DC's 6-3 vs RR at Arun Jaitley was opponent-specific. Entry 1 says "discount when contradicted by same-season results" — but the contradiction came from matches against OTHER teams, not the specific opponent in the H2H. When a team's general home record is poor but their venue H2H against the specific opponent is strong, Entry 1's discount should be reduced (perhaps 25% rather than 50%+) because the H2H captures a persistent matchup dynamic that the general record cannot. DC's 7-3 vs RR at this venue now spans multiple squads and years — this is Entry 10 territory (persistent venue dynamics).

- **Update Entry 19 — first application, immediately validated.** DC's improving form (beat RR May 1, beat PBKS May 11) was correctly identified as masking a positive trajectory within a poor headline record. DC then won their third in four matches. The rule's mechanism was confirmed: when the recent sub-window (last 3-4 matches) diverges from the full window (last 8), the sub-window dominates. The form trajectory was partly tactical (Porel replacing Nissanka, new lineup gelling) — not just win/loss noise. Future applications should look for tactical changes within the sub-window as strengthening evidence that the trajectory is real rather than random.

- **New pattern: Entry 18 (ceiling assessment) applies to bowlers, not just batters.** Starc's 4/40 (including 3-in-4-balls) was the match's decisive moment. His international profile (World Cup final winner, 143 T20I wickets, multiple 3+ wicket hauls in T20) indicated a ceiling far above what DC's aggregate bowling stats (Kuldeep 10.3 economy, Mukesh 10.27) suggest at team level. When one world-class bowler is present in an otherwise "porous" attack, the team's bowling floor is low (when Starc has an off day) but the ceiling is high (when Starc produces a spell like this). Entry 18's framework — "weight profile over season stats for ceiling assessment" — should be extended to bowlers, particularly in T20 where one great over can collapse an innings. Season team-level bowling averages mask individual spike probability.

- **When Entry 8 doesn't trigger (gap <3pp), check whether the ABSENCE of a gap is itself suspicious.** The market had DC at 43.5%. The memo's base-rate estimate was ~44% DC. The gap was <3pp, so Entry 8 ("lean toward base-rate when divergence exists") didn't trigger. But the analytical evidence pointed toward DC being higher than 44% (venue H2H, recent trajectory, Rahul's form). The absence of a gap should not be treated as confirmation that the market is correct — it might mean the base-rate estimate is being pulled toward the market by insufficient confidence in the contrarian signals. When multiple independent factors all point toward the same team being underpriced, and the market agrees with the base-rate estimate, ask: "is my base-rate estimate being anchored BY the market?" rather than "does my estimate confirm the market?"
